Chunk 1 of 4

The Punitive Campaign: Suetonius Unbowed

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Read the first paragraph aloud and model critical thinking: “Notice the contradiction here — Suetonius is punishing the very people he needs to govern. He’s destroying the crops they need to survive, which means they can’t pay taxes either. This is a short-term emotional response that creates long-term problems. Can you think of other historical examples where a ruler’s revenge backfired?”

The military victory did not end the crisis. Suetonius Paulinus, with the methodical ruthlessness that characterised his approach throughout the revolt, launched a systematic punitive campaign against the tribes that had participated in the rebellion. He devastated their lands, burned crops, and destroyed settlements. Roman reinforcements arrived from Germany — two thousand legionaries, eight auxiliary cohorts, and a thousand cavalry — strengthening the battered garrison.

The consequences for the native population were severe. Tacitus records that those tribes that had remained wavering or had been hostile were reduced by fire and sword. The deliberate destruction of crops, however, created a problem Suetonius may not have anticipated: winter was approaching, and the people he was punishing had not planted crops because they had expected victory and Roman plunder to supply their needs. The result was widespread famine.

Suetonius’s approach was strategically problematic. A province stripped of its agricultural base, its towns destroyed, and its population decimated by war, famine, and reprisal could not pay taxes, supply the army, or maintain the peace. The proconsul was punishing a province he needed to govern. His superiors in Rome began to take notice.

Classicianus and the Call for Conciliation

Teacher — Guided Reading (‘We Do’)

Have students read this section silently. Then use think-pair-share: “Turn to your partner and discuss: Why was it significant that Classicianus was of Gaulish origin?” (60 seconds). Cold call 2–3 pairs.

Emphasise the archaeological discovery of Classicianus’s tomb — this is a rare moment where a person named in a text is confirmed by physical evidence.

The new procurator appointed to replace Catus Decianus was Gaius Julius Alpinus Classicianus — a man of Gaulish origin whose own people had experienced Roman conquest and whose approach to provincial governance was fundamentally different from his predecessor’s.

Classicianus sent a private report to Rome recommending that Suetonius be replaced. The report, as summarised by Tacitus, argued that the ongoing war would never be brought to a peaceful conclusion while Suetonius remained governor, because Suetonius’s personal pride bound up his own failures with the outcome of the war — any peace would look like an admission that the revolt had not been fully crushed. A fresh governor without this history, Classicianus argued, could treat the defeated tribes with mercy and restore stability.

Archaeological Discovery

The tomb of Classicianus himself was discovered in Roman London in 1852 and again in 1935. Fragments of his inscribed tombstone, erected by his wife Julia Pacata, are now displayed in the British Museum. The tomb represents one of the rare moments when an individual named in a written source is directly confirmed by archaeological evidence — an extraordinary convergence of history and archaeology.

The emperor Nero sent a freedman, Polyclitus, to investigate the situation. Tacitus notes with dry irony that Polyclitus, an imperial freedman of no military experience, was treated with enormous deference by Roman soldiers who had just defeated Boudicca’s hundreds of thousands, but that the British tribespeople found the sight of a freedman commanding generals and armies incomprehensible. Polyclitus’s report appears to have supported Classicianus: Suetonius was eventually recalled, following a pretext involving the loss of some ships, and replaced by the more conciliatory Publius Petronius Turpilianus.

The New Policy: From Repression to Reconstruction

Teacher — Modelling: Source Analysis

Before reading the Tacitus source below, set up the context: “Tacitus admired military toughness. He’s going to describe the new peaceful policy — but pay attention to how he describes it. What words reveal his bias?” After reading, model identifying the phrase ‘dishonorable name of peace’ as revealing Tacitus’s senatorial bias toward military glory.

Turpilianus adopted a policy of non-provocation and reconstruction. Tacitus, who deeply admired Suetonius and his military hardness, is scornful — he describes Turpilianus as exchanging “their own slaughter for the dishonorable name of peace.” But this reflects Tacitus’s senatorial bias toward military glory rather than an objective assessment of good governance.

The shift in policy had practical wisdom behind it. Rome had learned from the revolt that the systematic humiliation of provincial elites — taking their land, flogging their queens, assaulting their daughters — risked producing exactly the kind of mass uprising that had nearly torn the province from the empire. The new approach emphasised co-opting the native nobility into Roman administrative structures, encouraging the construction of Roman-style public buildings and amenities, and moderating the financial exploitation that had contributed to the revolt.

The Agricolan period of Roman Britain, which followed in the late first century AD, represents the flowering of this new approach. Under Gnaeus Julius Agricola (governor AD 77–84), Roman Britain saw significant urban expansion, the construction of fora and basilicae, the encouragement of Latin education among the British elite, and major military advances into northern Britain and Scotland.

Primary Source

Provenance: Tacitus, Agricola 21 (c. AD 98). The Agricola is a biographical work about Tacitus’s father-in-law, who served as governor of Britain (AD 77–84). As a celebratory biography, it presents Agricola’s policies — including Romanisation — in a deliberately favourable light. Translation: Harold Mattingly, revised S.A. Handford (Penguin, 1970).

“Agricola gave private encouragement and official assistance to the building of temples, public squares, and good houses… He educated the sons of the chiefs in the liberal arts and expressed a preference for British ability over the trained skills of Gauls. The result was that instead of loathing the Latin language they became eager to speak it effectively.”

Historian’s note: This passage is one of antiquity’s clearest descriptions of deliberate Romanisation — the systematic replacement of native culture with Roman customs, language, and urban life. Tacitus presents this positively, framing it as civilising progress. However, modern historians read it with considerable irony: Tacitus himself, just a few chapters later in the same work, has the Caledonian chieftain Calgacus describe Roman rule as “they create a desolation and call it peace.” The Agricola therefore contains within itself both the Roman justification for cultural imperialism and a sharp critique of it.

The Legacy for Native Britons

Teacher — Scaffolding: Two Sides

This section presents a nuanced argument — the revolt had both devastating short-term consequences and paradoxically positive long-term effects. Use think-pair-share: “With your partner, create a two-column table: ‘Short-term consequences’ and ‘Long-term consequences.’ Fill it in from the text.” (2 minutes). Cold call 3 pairs to share one entry each.

The long-term consequences of the Boudiccan revolt for the native British population were deeply ambiguous. In the short term, the defeat brought devastating reprisals, famine, and the loss of a generation of warriors. The Iceni as a distinct political entity effectively ceased to exist after the revolt — there is no further mention of them as an independent tribe in Roman sources.

In the longer term, the revolt may have paradoxically improved the situation of the remaining British population. The recognition in Rome that systematic exploitation could destabilise the province led to more careful management of provincial affairs. The new class of Romanised Britons that emerged in the late first and second centuries AD — builders, administrators, soldiers, merchants — found genuine opportunities for advancement within the imperial system.

Yet this assimilation came at a profound cultural cost. Celtic language, religion, and oral tradition were progressively marginalised. The Druids were suppressed. The bards who had kept Celtic memory alive faded from the historical record. What replaced them was Roman Latin culture — impressive, durable, but not the culture of the people who had followed Boudicca into battle.

Key Question

Was Romanisation a form of progress or a form of cultural destruction? This question has no simple answer — and the tension between the two views is central to understanding the legacy of Boudicca’s revolt.
