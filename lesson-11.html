Chunk 1 of 5

The Roman Situation Before the Battle

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Read the first paragraph aloud and model strategic thinking: “Notice how Suetonius had roughly ten thousand men. That sounds like a lot, but Boudicca’s army was many times larger. So Suetonius had to find a way to make the terrain work in his favour. Let’s see how he did that.”

Draw a simple diagram on the board showing the narrow defile with forest behind and open ground in front.

Suetonius Paulinus had retreated north and west with his available forces — approximately ten thousand men drawn from Legio XIV Gemina, Legio XX Valeria Victrix, and auxiliary units. He faced a critical shortage: Legio II Augusta, based at Exeter under the acting commander Poenius Postumus, refused to march to his support, a decision Postumus would later pay for with his own life by falling on his sword in shame. Legio IX was shattered. The governor had to fight with what he had.

Tacitus records that Suetonius carefully chose his battlefield. He selected a position in a narrow defile — a valley or pass — that had forest behind it and open ground in front, preventing any possibility of a flanking attack from the rear. This terrain choice was deliberate: it negated Boudicca’s overwhelming numerical advantage by funnelling the Celtic charge into a narrow front where numbers could not be brought to bear.

Modern historians and archaeologists have long debated the location of this final battle. The most widely accepted candidate is Mancetter (Manduessedum) in Warwickshire, near the modern town of Atherstone, which lies on Watling Street — the main Roman military road that would have been Suetonius’s line of march. Other proposed sites include the Midlands near Paulerspury and, more controversially, a site in north London. None has been definitively confirmed by archaeological evidence.

The Celtic Army: Numbers and Confidence

Teacher — Guided Reading (‘We Do’)

Have students read this section silently. Then use think-pair-share: “Turn to your partner and discuss: What mistake did Boudicca’s warriors make by bringing their families to watch the battle?” (60 seconds). Cold call 2–3 pairs to share.

By the time of the final confrontation, Boudicca’s army had swelled to enormous proportions, flush with the confidence of three complete victories and the plunder of three prosperous Roman towns. Tacitus gives an estimate of 230,000 warriors — a figure almost certainly exaggerated, as such an army would have been logistically impossible to supply and manoeuvre in ancient conditions. Even so, the Celtic force clearly vastly outnumbered the Roman army.

A detail recorded by both Tacitus and Cassius Dio that would prove fatal: Boudicca’s warriors had brought their families to watch the expected victory. Tacitus describes women and children positioned in wagons arranged along the edge of the plain at the open end of the defile — a measure intended to allow them to observe the Roman rout. These wagons, loaded with noncombatants and supplies, would block the Celtic line of retreat when the battle turned.

Boudicca herself, according to Tacitus, rode in a chariot from tribe to tribe before the battle, her daughters beside her, delivering a speech designed to rouse her warriors. Tacitus gives us a version of this speech — but we must remember that no Roman was present to record it. The speech is a literary construction, following the conventions of ancient historiography in which generals and commanders give speeches that reflect the author’s themes rather than actual words delivered.

Secondary Source

Provenance: Tacitus, Annals 14.35–36 (c. AD 98). Note that this speech was composed by Tacitus — it represents his literary reconstruction of what Boudicca might have said, not a verbatim record. Translation: A.J. Woodman (Penguin, 2004).

“Boudicca, riding in a chariot with her daughters in front of her, approached each tribe in turn. This was no time for the niceties of Roman warfare. She had good reason to avenge her kingdom’s seizure and her own flogging, but she was fighting for freedom and for justice for all Britons. She said that the gods favoured the just cause: one legion had already been routed; the rest were cowering in their camps. They would never stand the din and roar of so many thousands — not to mention her own war-cry. She urged them to think of how many of them there were, and why they were fighting.”

Historian’s note: This passage is one of the most debated in the entire Boudiccan tradition. Tacitus presents Boudicca addressing her troops before the final battle — but the speech is an inventio, a literary construction, not a transcript. Ancient historians regularly composed speeches they believed were plausible given the speaker’s character and circumstances. What makes this passage historically valuable is not what Boudicca said, but what Tacitus chose to put in her mouth: a leader framing the conflict as a defence of freedom, justice, and divine favour.

The Battle: Roman Tactics and Celtic Collapse

Teacher — Modelling: Tactical Reconstruction

Use the infographic below to walk through the battle step by step. Point to each phase as you explain it: (1) Romans hold position, (2) Celtic charge, (3) pilum volley, (4) wedge advance, (5) collapse into wagons. Ask students to narrate one step each using cold call.

Suetonius deployed his legionaries in a tight wedge formation in the mouth of the defile, with cavalry on the flanks. The Roman plan was simple but tactically brilliant: hold ground, absorb the Celtic charge, then advance in disciplined formation.

When Boudicca’s warriors charged, Suetonius ordered his men to stand still until the enemy was within range, then launch a volley of pila (heavy throwing spears). The pilum was designed with a soft iron shank that bent on impact, making shields impossible to use after the spear struck them. With their shields useless and the Roman wedge advancing into them in close formation, the Celtic warriors — accustomed to individual displays of bravery in open combat — could not operate effectively.

The Romans advanced in tight formation, shields locked, stabbing with their short gladii (swords) into the packed Celtic mass. As the Celtic front ranks buckled, those behind them pushed forward — not into victory, but into the crush. And then, catastrophically, those fleeing the Roman advance ran into their own wagons. The ring of wagons that had been positioned to give families a viewing platform now became a death trap. Hemmed in on all sides, the Celtic force was slaughtered with no means of escape.

Tacitus records 80,000 Celtic dead — again, an exaggerated figure serving rhetorical purposes — compared to roughly 400 Roman dead. The actual numbers cannot be verified, but the scale of the Roman victory was clearly decisive and comprehensive. The revolt was over.

The Death of Boudicca: A Historical Puzzle

Teacher — Scaffolding: Comparative Table

Walk through the comparison table below row by row. For each row, ask: “Which version do you find more convincing, and why?” Use think-pair-share (90 seconds) then cold call 3 pairs. Emphasise that historians cannot resolve this — neither account can be verified.

The fate of Boudicca is one of the small mysteries of ancient history. The two main sources disagree on how she died, and no archaeological evidence has been found to resolve the question.

Tacitus Cassius Dio
How she died Took poison to avoid capture (Annals XIV.37) Fell ill and died — no mention of poison (Roman History LXII.12)
Tone Dramatic but restrained; emphasises Boudicca’s personal agency and dignified resistance to the last More sensationalised; greater emphasis on spectacle, elaborate funeral rites, and Roman triumph
Reliability Writing ~50–55 years after the revolt; closer in time with access to official records and family accounts through Agricola Writing ~160 years after the revolt; likely dependent on Tacitus and earlier sources now lost; mediated through Xiphilinus’s 11th-century epitome
Purpose Portrays Boudicca as a tragic, dignified figure whose defeat reflects Roman misgovernance rather than Celtic inferiority; used to critique corrupt officials Uses her death to reinforce Rome’s inevitable dominance; the elaborate burial underscores the spectacle of defeated barbarian power

Note: Neither account can be verified independently. The absence of archaeological evidence for Boudicca’s burial site means the written sources remain the only evidence for the circumstances of her death.

Tacitus’s version — that Boudicca took poison rather than submit to Roman capture — has a literary parallel in the death of Cleopatra, another powerful woman who chose death over subjugation. Whether this reflects historical reality or literary convention is impossible to determine. Dio’s version is less dramatic but potentially more plausible: a military defeat of this scale, following weeks of intense campaigning, could easily lead to illness and death from exhaustion and exposure.

The location of Boudicca’s burial — if she received one — is entirely unknown. Various Victorian and modern claims of burial sites, including Hampstead Heath, and beneath Platform 8 at King’s Cross station, are folklore with no archaeological foundation. Her burial remains one of the most tantalising unsolved questions of Roman British archaeology.

Why Did Boudicca Lose?

Teacher — Gradual Release: I Do, We Do, You Do

  1. I Do: Model the first factor (terrain) yourself, explaining why it was decisive.
  2. We Do: Work through the second and third factors together as a class, taking suggestions from students.
  3. You Do: Students explain the fourth factor (army size as a problem) independently in their notes.

Check understanding after each step. Use cold call to share responses.

The military defeat of a numerically superior force by Roman discipline raises important questions about the nature of ancient warfare and the limits of Celtic military power. Several key factors contributed to the Celtic defeat:

1. Terrain advantage: Suetonius chose terrain that neutralised his numerical disadvantage, forcing Boudicca’s warriors into a frontal assault on a narrow front.

2. Roman tactical discipline: The ability to stand firm under a charge, execute a coordinated volley, and advance in close formation proved decisive against a Celtic style of fighting that relied on individual warrior courage and the shock of the initial charge.

3. The wagon trap: The wagons blocking Celtic retreat transformed a defeat into a massacre.

4. Logistical problems: The size of the Celtic army — while impressive — created difficulties: a huge force is difficult to feed, to manoeuvre, and to maintain in cohesion over time.

Historical Thinking

The Boudiccan revolt illustrates a pattern seen repeatedly in encounters between Rome and Celtic warriors: spectacular early success followed by decisive defeat when the Romans chose their ground and their moment carefully. Tactical superiority, not simply numerical advantage, determined outcomes in ancient warfare.
