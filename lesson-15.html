Chunk 1 of 5

Why Paragraph Structure Matters in Ancient History

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Start with a concrete example: “Imagine I asked you: ‘Why was Camulodunum attacked first?’ A descriptive answer tells me what happened. An analytical answer explains why it happened and what it reveals. The second kind is what gets you into Band 5 and 6.”

Write a one-sentence descriptive answer and a one-sentence analytical answer on the board, side by side.

Ancient History essays at NSW Stage 6 are not tests of how much information you have memorised. They are tests of how well you can use historical evidence to construct and support an argument. The most common reason students do not achieve high marks is not that they lack knowledge — it is that their knowledge is presented as description rather than analysis.

The difference between description and analysis is simple: description tells what happened; analysis explains why it happened, what it means, or what it reveals. A Band 6 essay in Ancient History is one that consistently moves from evidence to analysis — from “what” to “why” and “so what.”

The PEEL paragraph structure is a practical tool for building analytical paragraphs. It is not the only way to write well, but it provides a reliable framework that ensures every paragraph contains all the elements of strong historical writing.

The PEEL Structure

Teacher — Explicit Instruction

Walk through each letter slowly. For each one, ask: “Can someone give me an example of what this would look like in a Boudicca essay?” (cold call). Build understanding from concrete examples, not abstract definitions.

Letter Stands For What It Means
PPointThe main argument or claim of your paragraph. This should directly answer the question and be debatable (not just a statement of fact).
EEvidenceThe specific historical evidence that supports your point. This includes named primary sources, archaeological evidence, and relevant historical details.
EExplainYour analysis of the evidence — explaining what it means, why it matters, and how it supports your point.
LLinkA linking sentence that connects your paragraph back to the overall essay question and/or leads into the next paragraph.

Sequence the PEEL Paragraph

Put these parts of a PEEL paragraph in the correct order:

Explain — analyse the evidence and explain what it means
Point — state your main argument or claim
Link — connect back to the essay question or lead into the next paragraph
Evidence — provide specific historical evidence to support your point

Chunk 3 of 5

Building a Thesis Statement

Teacher — Modelling: Compare Weak and Strong

Display both thesis statements on the board. Read them aloud and think aloud about why the weak one fails and the strong one works:

  • “The weak one just lists factors — it doesn’t take a side.”
  • “The strong one uses the word ‘fundamentally’ — that’s a position. It says: this one thing matters more than the others.”
  • “The strong one also tells me what evidence it will use — that’s a roadmap.”

Before you can write PEEL paragraphs, you need a clear thesis — the central argument of your entire essay. A thesis statement should:

  • Directly answer the question (not just paraphrase it)
  • Be arguable — it must be a position that could be contested, not a simple fact
  • Be specific — it should indicate the key reasons or evidence you will use
  • Appear in your introduction and be reinforced in your conclusion

Sample question:

“To what extent was Boudicca’s revolt a product of Roman misgovernance?” (15 marks)

Weak thesis:

“The revolt was caused by many factors including Roman misgovernance and Celtic grievances.”

Why this is weak: It simply lists factors without arguing a position. It is not debatable.

Strong thesis:

“Although Celtic cultural resistance and tribal alliances were significant factors, the Boudiccan revolt was fundamentally a product of Roman misgovernance — specifically the systematic violation of the client kingdom arrangement under Prasutagus and the financial exploitation directed by Catus Decianus — without which the revolt could not have achieved its initial scale or intensity.”

Why this is strong: It takes a clear position (“fundamentally”), acknowledges complexity (“although”), specifies the evidence it will use, and makes an arguable claim that guides the essay.

A Worked PEEL Paragraph

Teacher — Modelling: Annotated Example (‘I Do’)

Display the worked example on the board. Read it aloud, pausing after each section to label it: “That’s the Point — notice how it directly answers the question.” “That’s the Evidence — see how the source is named?”

Use coloured highlighting (or underlining) to make each PEEL element visually distinct.

Question: “To what extent was Boudicca’s revolt a product of Roman misgovernance?”

P — Point:

Roman misgovernance was the primary cause of the Boudiccan revolt, as the systematic violation of the client kingdom arrangement created the conditions for mass rebellion.

E — Evidence:

Following the death of Prasutagus in c. AD 59, Roman officials ignored his will and seized Iceni lands as if they had been conquered territory. Tacitus records in Annals 14.31 that “the kingdom was pillaged by centurions, his house by slaves, as if they were the spoils of war.” Boudicca herself was flogged and her daughters violated.

E — Explain:

This evidence demonstrates that it was specifically the violation of a formal political agreement — the client kingdom arrangement — that triggered the revolt. The Iceni had cooperated with Rome under Prasutagus for approximately fifteen years; it was the sudden reversal of this arrangement, not merely general Roman oppression, that made revolt both politically justified and broadly supported. The participation of the Trinovantes, also driven by specific grievances over Camulodunum, reinforces the conclusion that Roman misgovernance was the decisive trigger.

L — Link:

While other factors — Celtic warrior tradition, economic grievances, and personal leadership — contributed to the revolt’s scale, the evidence strongly supports the conclusion that without Roman misgovernance of this specific kind, the revolt would not have occurred when and how it did.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Narration without analysis: Describing what happened in sequence without explaining what it means for the argument.

Unsupported claims: Making arguments without citing specific historical evidence. Every point needs evidence.

Burying the analysis: Spending most of the paragraph on evidence and only one sentence on analysis. The Explain section should be at least as long as the Evidence section.

Vague links: “This shows that Boudicca was important.” — Too vague. Link specifically back to the essay question.

Describing sources rather than using them: “Tacitus tells us that...” is weak. “Tacitus’s account of the flogging of Boudicca reveals that...” uses the source analytically.

Source Analysis Within Essays

Teacher — Gradual Release: I Do, We Do, You Do

  1. I Do: Read the weak source use aloud and explain why it is weak: “It just tells me what Tacitus said. It doesn’t analyse the source at all.”
  2. We Do: Read the strong source use together and identify what makes it analytical: evaluative language, provenance detail, judgement about the evidence.
  3. You Do: Students rewrite the weak example in their own words, adding source analysis.

In Ancient History essays, you are expected not just to cite sources but to analyse them — to comment on their nature, purpose, and reliability. This should be done briefly within the flow of your paragraph, not as a separate interruption.

Weak source use:

“Tacitus says in Annals 14.31 that Boudicca was flogged.”

Strong source use:

“Tacitus’s account in Annals 14.31 — written approximately 57 years after the revolt and drawing on sources that may have included eyewitness testimony — records that Boudicca was publicly flogged, a detail whose specificity and placement in the narrative suggests it reflects a genuine historical memory preserved in the Roman administrative record.”

Evaluate and debate

  1. Write two full PEEL paragraphs in response to the question: “To what extent was the Boudiccan revolt a product of Roman misgovernance?” Your paragraphs should argue competing considerations and use at least two named primary sources.
  2. Evaluate the PEEL framework as a tool for essay writing in Ancient History. What are its strengths and what are its limitations? When might a skilled writer deviate from it?

Develop your own annotated essay plan for a 600-word response to the question: “How useful are the accounts of Tacitus and Cassius Dio for understanding the causes and conduct of the Boudiccan revolt?” Your plan should include a thesis, topic sentences for each paragraph, key evidence, and anticipated analytical points.
