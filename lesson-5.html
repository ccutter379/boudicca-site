Who Was Tacitus?

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Think-aloud as you introduce Tacitus: “When I read that Tacitus was a Roman senator, I immediately wonder — whose side is he on? If he’s part of Rome’s ruling class, how might that shape the way he tells Boudicca’s story?”

Read the first two paragraphs aloud, then pause to check understanding before continuing.

Publius Cornelius Tacitus (c. AD 56 – c. AD 120) was a Roman senator, lawyer, and historian who is widely regarded as the greatest of all Roman historians. He was born in the reign of Nero and lived through the turbulent period of the Year of the Four Emperors (AD 69) before achieving his literary maturity under the relative stability of Trajan’s reign (AD 98–117).

Tacitus had a distinguished public career, serving as consul (the highest regular magistracy in Rome) and as governor of the province of Asia. He was deeply embedded in the senatorial class — the small elite that governed the Roman Empire — and his writing reflects the perspective of an educated Roman who had witnessed both the grandeur and the corruption of imperial rule at close range.

His most important historical works are the Histories (covering AD 69–96, the period from the Year of the Four Emperors to the death of Domitian) and the Annals (covering AD 14–68, from the death of Augustus to the death of Nero). Boudicca’s revolt falls within the Annals, which was written in the early second century AD, approximately fifty-seven years after the events it describes.

Tacitus’s Connection to Britain

Tacitus had a personal connection to Britain that distinguished him from most Roman writers who discussed the province. His father-in-law was Gnaeus Julius Agricola — one of the most celebrated governors of Roman Britain (AD 77–83/84) and the man who pushed Roman control to its greatest northern extent. Tacitus wrote a biography of Agricola (the Agricola) that includes valuable information about Roman Britain and suggests that he had direct access to eyewitness accounts of British affairs through his family connection.

This personal connection does not make Tacitus free of bias — if anything, his loyalty to Agricola’s memory and his critique of the emperors under whom Agricola served shaped his narrative. But it does mean that his information about British affairs was likely more detailed and accurate than what was available to most Roman writers.

Historian Profile: Tacitus

Full name: Publius Cornelius Tacitus

Born: c. AD 56–57, Gallia Narbonensis (southern France)

Died: c. AD 120

Role: Roman Senator · Lawyer · Consul (AD 97)

Language: Latin

Writing style: Compressed, moralising, ironic — literary history

Principal Works:

  • Annals (c. AD 117) — Covers the reigns of Tiberius, Claudius and Nero (AD 14–68). Books XIII–XIV contain the primary account of Boudicca’s revolt.
  • Histories (c. AD 100) — Covers the Year of the Four Emperors (AD 69) through the reign of Domitian.
  • Agricola (c. AD 98) — A biography of his father-in-law, Governor of Britain. Essential for understanding Tacitus’s direct access to eyewitness testimony.
  • Germania (c. AD 98) — An ethnographic study of Germanic tribes, used partly to contrast ‘barbarian’ virtues with Roman moral decline.

The Agricola Connection: Why Tacitus Matters for Boudicca

Tacitus was the son-in-law of Gnaeus Julius Agricola, who served as Governor of Britain (AD 77–84). As a young officer, Agricola served under Suetonius Paulinus during the Boudiccan revolt itself. This means Tacitus had access to a direct eyewitness of the revolt — giving his account in the Annals a level of proximity unavailable to later historians.

Boudiccan Revolt (c. AD 60–61) → Agricola serves under Paulinus as a junior officer, later Governor of Britain AD 77–84 → Tacitus, son-in-law of Agricola, writes the Annals c. AD 117, approximately 57 years after the revolt.

Tacitus on the Revolt: The Primary Sources

Teacher — Guided Reading (‘We Do’)

Read each source extract aloud to the class. After each one, pause and use think-pair-share: “Turn to your partner and identify one specific word or phrase that reveals Tacitus’s perspective.” (60 seconds). Cold call 2–3 pairs to share.

The following passages from Book XIV of Tacitus’s Annals cover the causes of the revolt, the attack on the three cities, and the final battle. Read them carefully, noting both what Tacitus tells us and how he tells it.

PRIMARY SOURCE — Tacitus, Annals, Book XIV, Chapters 29–37. Written c. AD 98. Translation by Alfred John Church and William Jackson Brodribb, 1876 (public domain). These chapters form the core ancient narrative of Boudicca’s revolt.

“In the consulship of Caesennius Paetus and Petronius Turpilianus [AD 61]… while Suetonius was thus employed, he received tidings of a sudden revolt in the province. Prasutagus, king of the Iceni, famed for his long prosperity, had made the emperor co-heir with his two daughters, under the impression that this token of submission, combined with his great wealth, would put his kingdom and household out of the reach of wrong. But the reverse was the result; so much so that his kingdom was plundered by centurions, his house by slaves, as if they were the spoils of war. First, his wife Boudicca was scourged and his daughters outraged. All the chief men of the Iceni, as if Rome had received the whole country as a gift, were stripped of their ancestral estates, and the king’s relatives were treated like slaves. Exasperated by these insults, and dreading still worse, since they were now reduced to the condition of a province, they flew to arms, and drew into the rebellion the Trinobantes [Trinovantes] and others who, not yet broken in to subjection, had agreed by secret conspiracies to reassert their freedom.”
Historian’s note: Note Tacitus’s key choices: he presents Prasutagus’s will as a “token of submission” (highlighting Iceni good faith), uses the verb “scourged” (a word used for criminal punishment, highlighting the dishonour), and describes the behaviour of Roman officials as treating the kingdom “as if it were the spoils of war” — i.e., as conquered enemies rather than voluntary allies. The phrase “drawn into the rebellion… who had agreed by secret conspiracies” suggests pre-planning, contradicting narratives of spontaneous revolt.

PRIMARY SOURCE — Tacitus, Annals, Book XIV, Chapter 33. Written c. AD 98. This passage describes the Roman civilian massacre during the sack of Londinium.

“Suetonius, however, with wonderful resolution, marched amidst a hostile population to Londinium, which, though undistinguished by the name of a colony, was much frequented by a number of merchants and trading vessels. Uncertain whether he should choose it as a seat of war, as he looked round on his scanty force of soldiers, and remembered with what a serious warning the rashness of Petilius had been punished, he resolved to save the province at the cost of a single town. Nor did the tears and weeping of the people, as they implored his aid, move him from giving the signal of departure and receiving into his army all who would go with him. Those who were chained to the spot by the weakness of their sex, or the infirmity of age, or the attachment to the place, were cut off by the enemy.”
Historian’s note: Tacitus presents Suetonius’s abandonment of Londinium as a rational military decision rather than a moral failure — “wonderful resolution” is the phrase he uses. This represents the Roman military perspective: the goal is to save the province (Roman control), not to protect individual civilians. The phrase “chained to the spot by the weakness of their sex” reflects Roman gender assumptions — Tacitus implies women stayed because they were physically or emotionally incapable of the rational choice of retreat.

PRIMARY SOURCE — Tacitus, Annals, Book XIV, Chapter 34. Written c. AD 98. This is Tacitus’s account of Boudicca’s speech to her forces before the final battle. Like all ancient battle speeches, it is a rhetorical construction by the historian, not a verbatim record.

“Boudicca, with her daughters before her in a chariot, went up to tribe after tribe, protesting that it was indeed usual for Britons to fight under the leadership of women. But now she was not fighting to recover her kingdom and plunder, but like one of the people, to avenge lost freedom, her scourged body, the outraged chastity of her daughters. Roman lust had gone so far that not even their persons, age or virginity, were left unpolluted. But heaven was on the side of a righteous vengeance; a legion which dared to face the armed host had been destroyed; the rest were skulking in their camps, or anxiously seeking a way of escape. They would not sustain even the din and the shout of so many thousands, much less their onslaught and their blows. If they would but consider the forces under arms, and the causes of the war, they would see that in that battle they must conquer or die. This was a woman’s resolve; as for the men, they might live and be slaves.”
Historian’s note: This speech is one of the most famous passages in ancient history. Tacitus constructs it to serve multiple purposes: it establishes Boudicca’s moral authority (she fights for justice, not plunder), acknowledges the “unusual” nature of female leadership in Roman eyes, and ends with a stinging rebuke aimed at Roman men hiding in their camps. The final line — “This was a woman’s resolve; as for the men, they might live and be slaves” — is a masterpiece of rhetorical inversion: a woman is more courageous than Rome’s professional soldiers. Whether Boudicca actually said anything like this is impossible to know.

Key Biases & Analytical Perspectives

Teacher — Scaffolding (‘We Do’)

Work through each bias category together. For each, ask students: “Can you find a specific example of this bias in the source extracts we just read?”

Think-pair-share for each category (30 seconds thinking, 30 seconds sharing with partner). Cold call pairs to report back.

Tacitus was a brilliant historian — but he was not neutral. His account of Boudicca’s revolt is shaped by several identifiable biases and literary conventions that students must recognise and analyse.

Tacitus’s Purpose: The Moral Historian

Tacitus wrote history with a clear moral and political purpose. He believed that historians had a duty to record the deeds of great men so they could be remembered for praise or blame — and to expose the moral decay that he saw as threatening the Roman state. His target was not merely individual corrupt emperors but the entire system of imperial rule, which he believed had corrupted Roman virtues by replacing republican competition and senatorial responsibility with autocratic power.

Boudicca’s revolt served Tacitus’s purpose perfectly. The revolt was caused by Roman injustice — the greed and brutality of Roman officials — and nearly destroyed Rome’s most prestigious recent conquest. By presenting Celtic leaders (and Boudicca in particular) as noble figures driven to revolt by Roman excess, Tacitus could shame Roman readers without directly attacking Rome itself. Boudicca becomes a mirror: her courage highlights Roman cowardice; her justice highlights Roman corruption; her freedom highlights Roman servitude.

This technique — using “barbarian” virtue to criticise Roman vice — was a recognised literary tradition. Tacitus also used it in his ethnographic essay Germania, where the moral simplicity of the Germanic peoples is used to critique Roman luxury and decadence. It does not mean Tacitus actually respected Celtic peoples as equals, but it does mean that his positive portrayals of Boudicca serve his own rhetorical agenda, not historical objectivity.

Senatorial Bias

As a Roman senator, Tacitus was deeply hostile to autocratic emperors — particularly Nero and Domitian — who undermined Senate authority. His account of the revolt is partly a critique of Neronian misgovernance in Britain. Students should ask: is Tacitus blaming the Romans as a moral argument about bad emperors, rather than simply recording events?

The ‘Noble Barbarian’ Trope

Tacitus frequently portrays non-Romans — Britons, Germans, Celts — as more virtuous, courageous and morally pure than Romans. This is a rhetorical device to shame Rome’s own moral decline. Boudicca’s eloquent speech before battle (Annals 14.35) is almost certainly composed by Tacitus, not a verbatim record. Her ‘noble’ qualities serve his argument, not historical accuracy.

Moralising History

Tacitus explicitly states that history’s purpose is to record virtuous acts and warn against evil. He is not simply a neutral recorder of facts — he selects, shapes and moralises. Every detail in his Boudicca narrative serves a larger argument about Roman governance, military virtue, and the consequences of greed and cruelty.

Cultural Superiority

Despite his sympathy for Boudicca’s cause, Tacitus maintains a Roman lens throughout. He views Britons as civilisationally inferior — people who need Roman humanitas (culture/civilisation). Famously in Agricola 21, he notes that Britons adopted Roman dress, baths and banquets — then adds, with biting irony, that they called this ‘civilisation’ when it was really ‘enslavement’.

Gender Assumptions

Tacitus’s portrayal of Boudicca is shaped by Roman assumptions about female leadership: her rule is presented as unusual, requiring explanation. He emphasises her physical appearance and her role as an outraged mother rather than a military commander in her own right. Modern historians (e.g. Hingley & Unwin, 2005) argue his portrayal tells us more about Roman gender ideology than about Boudicca herself.
