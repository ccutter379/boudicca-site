Caesar’s Expeditions to Britain (55–54 BC)

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Set the scene before reading: “Almost a hundred years before the actual invasion, Julius Caesar came to Britain twice. He didn’t conquer it — but he opened the door. Think of it like a scout checking out the territory before the army arrives.”

Read the first paragraph aloud, then pause to check understanding before continuing.

Julius Caesar mounted two expeditions to Britain — in 55 BC with two legions and in 54 BC with five legions. He did not conquer Britain; on both occasions he withdrew after establishing diplomatic relationships and extracting promises of tribute from some tribes. Caesar’s motivations were partly strategic (punishing tribes that were assisting Gaulish resistance), partly political (demonstrating to the Roman Senate that he was expanding Rome’s reach to the very edges of the known world), and partly economic (Britain was rumoured to have deposits of gold, pearls, and valuable slaves).

Caesar’s expeditions established the template for Roman engagement with Britain. They demonstrated that Britain was militarily accessible and economically exploitable, and they created a network of diplomatic relationships between Rome and certain British tribes — particularly those in south-eastern Britain who benefited from trade with the Roman world.

Primary Source

Provenance: Julius Caesar, De Bello Gallico (The Gallic Wars), Book IV, Chapter 20. Written c. 52–51 BC. Caesar’s account of his first expedition to Britain.

“The interior of Britain is inhabited by those who themselves say that, according to tradition, they were born in the island itself; the maritime part is inhabited by those who had passed over from the country of the Belgae for the purpose of plunder and making war… The most civilised of all these nations are they who inhabit Kent, which is entirely a maritime district, nor do they differ much from the Gallic customs.”

Historian’s note: Caesar’s language is revealing. He defines “civilised” by proximity to Mediterranean (Roman/Gallic) customs — those who trade with Rome are “civilised”; those inland are not. This reveals how Romans used the concept of civilisation as a justification for conquest and intervention.

The Claudian Invasion (AD 43)

Teacher — Guided Reading (‘We Do’)

Read this section together as a class. Pause after the first paragraph and ask: “Why did Claudius personally need a military triumph?” (cold call). This connects to understanding how Roman politics drove military decisions.

Think-pair-share after reading the Battle of the Medway: “Why do you think the Romans targeted the Britons’ horses and chariot ponies first?” (60 seconds).

Almost a century after Caesar, the Emperor Claudius launched a full-scale invasion of Britain in AD 43. His motivations included a desire for military prestige to strengthen his political position in Rome (Claudius had come to power in unusual circumstances and needed a military triumph), the economic attractions of Britain’s mineral resources and slaves, and the request for intervention by Verica, a pro-Roman British king who had been expelled by the Catuvellaunian ruler Caratacus.

The invasion force comprised four legions (approximately 20,000 legionary troops) plus auxiliary units — a total force of around 40,000 soldiers under the command of Aulus Plautius. Landing in what is now Kent, the Romans faced resistance from a confederation of British tribes led by Caratacus and his brother Togodumnus.

Battle of the Medway (AD 43)

At the River Medway (modern Kent/Essex), the Romans faced a large British force holding the opposite bank. The battle lasted two days — an unusually long engagement that suggests fierce resistance. Roman commander Aulus Plautius used Batavian auxiliaries (soldiers from the Rhine delta, skilled at swimming in full armour) to cross the river first, targeting the Britons’ horses and chariot ponies. Regular infantry then crossed on temporary bridges. The victory opened the road to Camulodunum (modern Colchester), the most important political centre in south-eastern Britain.

Emperor Claudius himself travelled to Britain to be present for the fall of Camulodunum — a politically calculated act. He spent only sixteen days in Britain but was able to claim the prestige of a triumph over the island at the edge of the world. By the end of AD 43, Rome controlled most of south-eastern Britain, and the process of transforming the conquered territory into a Roman province had begun.

What Was Romanisation?

Teacher — Modelling: I Do, We Do, You Do

I Do: Model with a modern analogy: “Imagine a powerful country conquered Australia and said: you must speak their language, follow their laws, worship their leaders, and rebuild your towns to look like theirs. That’s essentially what Romanisation was.”

We Do: Read the table together. Think-pair-share: “Turn to your partner — which method of Romanisation do you think would cause the most resentment, and why?” (90 seconds, then cold call 2–3 pairs.) Follow up with a retrieval gesture vote: “True or false: Romanisation was always forced on local peoples. Thumbs up for true, down for false.” (Answer: false — some local elites actively adopted Roman culture.) If fewer than 80% correct, revisit the accommodation vs resistance table.

You Do: Students read the text and identify one example of Romanisation that was voluntary and one that was imposed.

Romanisation was the process by which conquered peoples adopted Roman cultural practices, language, institutions, and ways of life. It was not always coerced — many local elites actively embraced Roman culture because it offered access to power, wealth, and status within the new imperial system. However, Romanisation was also imposed, particularly in areas like religion, law, and urban planning, and it often came at the cost of traditional practices and identities.

Method How It Worked
Latin language Latin became the language of administration, law, commerce, and elite culture. Local leaders were expected to conduct official business in Latin.
Urban planning The Romans built or expanded towns with a forum (civic centre), basilica (law courts), public baths, amphitheatre, and a grid-pattern street plan.
Roman law Roman legal codes replaced tribal customary law. Property rights, inheritance, contracts, and taxation were restructured according to Roman principles.
The Imperial Cult Worship of the emperor as a divine figure. Temples were built and participation in rituals was expected. This directly challenged Celtic religious beliefs.
Veteran colonies Retired legionaries were settled on land confiscated from local peoples. Colonies served as models of Roman life and permanent military garrisons.

The Temple of Claudius and Veteran Colonies

Teacher — Scaffolding

This section covers two of the most significant causes of Boudicca’s revolt. Ask students to read the section and underline or highlight any phrase that shows how the Romans mistreated local peoples. Then cold call 3–4 students to share what they underlined.

Think-pair-share: “Imagine you are a Trinovantes farmer whose land has just been taken by a retired Roman soldier. How would you feel? What would you do?” (90 seconds).

Nowhere was Roman cultural imposition more viscerally felt than at Camulodunum. The Temple of the Deified Claudius — a massive structure on an elevated podium, dominating the new Roman colony — demanded that local peoples worship a Roman emperor as a god. For Celtic peoples whose religious practices were deeply rooted in nature, tribal ancestors, and the authority of Druids, this was not merely politically offensive — it was a profound sacrilege.

Tacitus records that local peoples were forced to fund the temple and to provide priests for its rituals, creating an ongoing economic drain on the community. When Boudicca’s forces attacked Camulodunum, the Temple of Claudius was their primary target — and the last defenders barricaded themselves inside it for two days before being overwhelmed.

Veteran Colonies: Flashpoints of Resentment

The establishment of veteran colonies like Camulodunum (AD 49) was one of the most significant causes of provincial discontent. The colonists — retired soldiers who had completed twenty-five years of military service — were granted land confiscated from local peoples, including the Trinovantes tribe who had long inhabited the area. The veterans treated the local population with open contempt, evicting people from their homes, seizing additional lands, and using the authority of Roman citizenship to act with impunity.

Tacitus writes that the veterans “drove people from their homes, ejected them from their lands, called them captives and slaves.” The Trinovantes, who had initially submitted peacefully to Roman rule, found themselves dispossessed and humiliated. Their burning grievance against the Camulodunum colony would make them Boudicca’s most willing allies when revolt began.

Key Cause

The combination of land theft by veterans, forced funding of the Temple of Claudius, economic exploitation through loans and taxation, and the complete disregard for local customs and rights created a “powder keg” of resentment that Boudicca’s personal humiliation would ignite.

Primary Source

Provenance: Tacitus, Annals, Book XIV, Chapter 31. Written c. AD 98. This passage describes the grievances that fuelled Boudicca’s revolt, written from the perspective of a Roman senator critical of imperial excess.

“The kingdom of Prasutagus, who had long been a king celebrated for his wealth… was plundered by centurions, his house by slaves, as if they were the spoils of war. His wife Boudicca was scourged, his daughters ravished. All the chief men of the Iceni, as if Rome had received the whole country as a gift, were stripped of their ancestral estates, and the king’s relatives were treated like slaves. Exasperated by these insults, and dreading still worse, since they were now reduced to the condition of a province, they flew to arms.”

Historian’s note: This passage is the most important literary source for the causes of Boudicca’s revolt. Note that Tacitus presents Roman officials as acting unjustly — his purpose is partly to critique Roman governance.

Celtic Responses to Romanisation

Teacher — Independent Reading (‘You Do’)

Students read the comparison table independently. After reading, use think-pair-share: “Turn to your partner and identify one advantage and one disadvantage of accommodating Roman rule.” (60 seconds). Cold call 2–3 pairs.

Key question to pose: “What does the last row of the table tell us about how much protection accommodation actually offered?”

Celtic peoples responded to Romanisation in different ways. Some chose accommodation; others chose resistance. The table below compares these two responses.

Accommodation Resistance
Some Celtic elites accepted Roman citizenship and adopted Latin, Roman dress, and Roman administrative roles. Others, particularly in rural areas and among those dispossessed by colonisation, maintained traditional practices and resisted assimilation.
Roman culture brought real advantages: access to trade networks, protection under Roman law, and social status. Resistance was sometimes practical (maintaining Druidic traditions in secret) and sometimes violent (Boudicca’s revolt of AD 60/61).
Some tribal leaders became Roman magistrates (decuriones), managing local affairs under Roman oversight. Tribal leaders who resisted were stripped of authority and property, their lands confiscated, their customs suppressed.
Cultural accommodation was encouraged by Roman policy, which preferred indirect control through compliant local elites. The fate of the Iceni and Trinovantes showed that even accommodation offered no real protection when Roman officials chose to ignore their own laws.

Analyse and explain

  1. Explain why the establishment of veteran colonies at Camulodunum created resentment among the Trinovantes tribe.
  2. Analyse how the Imperial Cult conflicted with Celtic religious practices and why this conflict mattered politically.
  3. Using Tacitus’s account of Boudicca’s treatment as evidence, assess the extent to which Roman officials were responsible for provoking the revolt.

Sentence starters to help structure your response:

  • One reason this is significant is…
  • This source/evidence suggests that…
  • This can be explained by…
  • A key example of this is… which demonstrates…

Paragraph frame:

[Point] One important factor was… [Evidence] This is supported by… who states/shows that… [Explain] This evidence reveals that… because… [Link] Therefore, this demonstrates that…

Evaluate and debate

  1. Evaluate the extent to which Romanisation was a policy of “civilisation” or a policy of cultural imperialism. Use specific examples from Roman Britain to support your argument.
  2. How far did Roman policies in Britain reflect a fundamental contradiction between Roman claims to bring law and order and the actual treatment of client peoples? Discuss with reference to the evidence.
  3. To what extent was Boudicca’s revolt the inevitable consequence of Roman provincial policy, rather than the result of individual decisions by particular Roman officials?

Essay structure guide:

  • Introduction: Define key terms, state your argument clearly
  • Body paragraphs: Use PEEL — Point, Evidence, Explain, Link
  • Conclusion: Restate your argument, synthesise (don’t just repeat)

Evaluative starters: “To a significant/limited extent…” “While X is important, Y was more significant because…” “The evidence suggests that… however…”
