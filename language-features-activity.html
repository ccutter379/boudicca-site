Quick controls:
A

Reporting Verbs

How writers introduce a source quote

What is a reporting verb?

A reporting verb is the word that tells the reader who said something. It comes just before a quote.

Examples:
  • Tacitus records that… (sounds factual)
  • Cassius Dio reports that… (similar — neutral)
  • Tacitus describes how… (more vivid)
Why it matters: The verb you choose tells the reader how confident you are in the source. Words like records sound reliable. Words like claims sound doubtful.
1In sentence [5], Tacitus is about to give a quote about Prasutagus's will. What reporting verb does the writer use?
Hint: Look just after the word “Tacitus”.
Answer: records
Why it works: ‘Records’ is neutral — it makes Tacitus sound like a reliable witness who just wrote down what happened, without judging it.
2In sentence [10], Cassius Dio is the source. What reporting verb is used?
Hint: Look just after “Cassius Dio”.
Answer: reports
Why it works: ‘Reports’ is similar to ‘records’ — neutral and factual. The writer is treating Cassius Dio as a trustworthy source.
3In sentence [11], Tacitus tells us about Decianus's centurions. What reporting verb is used?
Answer: describes
Why it works: ‘Describes’ is slightly more vivid than ‘records’. It suggests Tacitus painted a picture for us — almost like a story.
4In sentence [16], Tacitus is the source again. What's the reporting verb?
Answer: writes
Why it works: ‘Writes’ is plain and straightforward. It just says what Tacitus put down on the page — no judgement either way.
5In sentence [27], the writer brings in modern historians (not an ancient source). What TWO-word phrase introduces their view?
Hint: Two short words, side by side.
Answer: have called
Why it works: ‘Have called’ is a different kind of reporting — it brings in a group (historians) and tells us what name they've given something (‘a perfect storm’). Useful when you want to bring in modern scholars' views.
B

Cohesive Devices

Connectives that link ideas together

What is a cohesive device?

A cohesive device is a small word (or short phrase) that joins two ideas together. It tells the reader how the next idea connects to the last one.

5 main types:
  • Contrast (opposite is coming): While, Although, Despite, yet
  • Adding (more of the same): Equally, Beyond, Also
  • Cause (this caused that): By, Because, So
  • Time (when something happened): At the same time, Finally
  • What-if (imagine if not): Without, Had not
Why it matters: Without these little words, your sentences feel like a list. With them, your essay flows and your argument is easy to follow.
1The writer wants to say: “one cause was personal, BUT something deeper caused the revolt.” What 5-letter word starts sentence [2]?
Answer: While (Contrast)
Why it works: ‘While’ tells the reader: “yes, one thing is true — but something more important is coming.” It's a perfect way to concede a small point before pushing your main argument.
2The writer wants to say “Prasutagus tried to be diplomatic, but Rome ignored him.” What word starts sentence [6]?
Hint: It means “even though”.
Answer: Despite (Contrast)
Why it works: ‘Despite’ sets up a contrast. It admits one thing is true (Prasutagus's diplomacy) before showing the opposite happened (Rome refused).
3The writer starts Body 2 (economic causes) by tying it back to the thesis (Roman misgovernance). What 2-word phrase starts sentence [9]?
Hint: Two words. The first word sounds like “the same amount”.
Answer: Equally rooted (Adding)
Why it works: ‘Equally rooted’ tells the reader: “this economic cause has the SAME deep roots as the political cause I just discussed.” It links Body 2 to Body 1 AND to the thesis.
4In Body 3 (cultural causes), the writer wants to widen the focus from just the Iceni to other tribes. What 3-word phrase starts sentence [15]?
Answer: Beyond the Iceni (Adding / Spatial)
Why it works: ‘Beyond the Iceni’ is a clever connective — it tells the reader, “we've talked about the Iceni already, now we're going wider to other tribes.” It opens up the scope of the argument.
5Body 4 (personal causes) is the climax — the trigger that set the revolt off. What 3-word phrase starts sentence [21]?
Hint: Starts with “What”.
Answer: What finally converted (Time / Sequence)
Why it works: ‘What finally converted’ tells the reader: “after all this build-up, here's the moment that turned grievance into action.” It signals the climax of the essay.
6In sentence [29], the writer asks the reader to imagine: “what if the structural injustice hadn't happened?” What word starts the sentence?
Hint: 7 letters. Means “if not for”.
Answer: Without (What-if / Counterfactual)
Why it works: ‘Without’ is a powerful analytical word. It lets you argue by imagining the cause didn't exist: “if X didn't happen, then Y wouldn't have happened either.” That's how historians prove causation.
C

Nominalisation

Turning action words into thing words

What is nominalisation?

Nominalisation is when you turn a VERB (an action word) into a NOUN (a thing word). It sounds tricky but you do it all the time!

Examples:
  • humiliate (verb) → humiliation (noun)
  • annex (verb) → annexation (noun)
  • exploit (verb) → exploitation (noun)
  • cruel (adjective) → cruelty (noun)
How to spot one: Look at the word ending. Most nominalisations end in: -tion · -sion · -ment · -ity · -ance · -ence.
Why writers do this: It makes writing more formal. Compare these:
  • “The Romans humiliated her” (everyday talk)
  • “the humiliation of Boudica” (academic essay)
The second one shifts focus from people doing things to the IDEA of the thing. That's the academic voice.
1In sentence [2], find the noun that comes from the verb humiliate.
Answer: humiliation
Why: The verb humiliate + the ending -tion = the noun humiliation. It now refers to the EVENT of being humiliated, not the action.
2In sentence [2], find the noun that means “the act of taking over (a kingdom)”. (It comes from a verb that starts with “annex”.)
Answer: annexation
Why: Annex (verb — to absorb a territory) + -ation = annexation. This is a key concept word for Roman expansion.
3In sentence [2], find a word ending in -tion that means “the act of using someone unfairly”.
Answer: exploitation
Why: Exploit (verb — to use unfairly) + -ation = exploitation. The noun packs the whole idea into one word.
4In sentence [19], find the noun that comes from the verb destroy.
Hint: Ends in -tion.
Answer: destruction
Why: Destroy (verb) → destruction (noun). Notice the spelling changes a little — that's normal for nominalisation.
5In sentence [25], find the noun that comes from the verb rebel.
Answer: rebellion
Why: Rebel (verb — to fight against authority) → rebellion (noun — the act/event of rebelling). Ends in -ion, a common ending.
D

Evaluative Language

Words that show what the writer thinks

What is evaluative language?

Evaluative language is when the writer uses words that show what they think — words that judge or rank something.

Three kinds:
  • Strong adverbs: fundamentally, ultimately, deeply
  • Ranking words: most, first, biggest, key
  • Judgement adjectives: decisive, devastating, calculated, significant
Why it matters: If your essay question says “assess” or “evaluate”, you must use this kind of language. Without it, you're just describing facts — not making an argument. Markers want you to take a position.
1In sentence [2], find the TWO words that show the writer believes Roman causes were the deepest reason for the revolt.
Hint: An adverb + a verb. Look near “the revolt was…”.
Answer: fundamentally caused
Why: ‘Fundamentally’ means “at the very deepest level”. The writer isn't just saying the Roman actions ‘caused’ the revolt — they're saying it was THE root cause. This is stronger than just saying ‘led to’.
2In sentence [3] (the thesis), find TWO words that mean “in the end, this was what mattered most”.
Answer: ultimately decisive
Why: ‘Ultimately’ = in the end / finally. ‘Decisive’ = the thing that settled it. Together, they make a strong final judgement — perfect for an ‘assess’ essay.
3In sentence [4], find the 4-word phrase that ranks the political cause above all others.
Answer: first and most structural
Why: This is a ranking phrase. ‘First’ = the most important. ‘Most structural’ = the deepest in the system. The writer is making a clear judgement about which cause matters most.
4In sentence [28] (the judgement), find the TWO-word phrase that names the most important cause.
Answer: most significant
Why: ‘Most significant’ ranks this cause above all the others. It's a key phrase for delivering the final judgement in an ‘assess’ essay.
5In sentence [24], find the TWO words that describe how severe the impact on Iceni honour was.
Answer: culturally devastating
Why: ‘Devastating’ = completely destructive. ‘Culturally’ = about a society's beliefs and identity. Together, these words tell the reader the assault was not just bad — it broke something deep.
E

Subject-Specific Terminology

Special words for the topic

What is subject-specific terminology?

Subject-specific terminology is the special vocabulary that experts use. For Ancient History, these are Roman or Celtic words you wouldn't use in everyday speech.

Some Boudica-era terms:
  • client kingdom = a friendly kingdom under Roman protection (not directly ruled)
  • procurator = a Roman tax official
  • imperial cult = forced worship of the Roman emperor as a god
  • stuprum = a Latin legal word for a serious crime against a citizen
  • Druid groves = sacred forests where Celtic priests held ceremonies
Why it matters: Using these words shows you really understand the topic. It's one of the fastest ways to lift a B-grade essay to an A-grade.
1In sentence [8], find the TWO words that describe what the Iceni kingdom was before Rome took it over. (Means: friendly kingdom, kept its king, paid Rome tribute.)
Answer: client kingdom
Definition: A semi-independent kingdom that kept its monarch but was subordinate to Rome. When the king died, Rome usually took over — which is exactly what triggered the revolt.
2In sentence [9], find the Roman job title of Catus Decianus.
Hint: It has a capital letter — like a job title would.
Answer: Procurator
Definition: A Roman financial official — basically a chief tax collector. Decianus held this role in Britain and his greedy actions provoked the revolt.
3In sentence [10], find the Roman word for the currency Seneca loaned out.
Answer: sesterces
Definition: The basic unit of Roman currency. Seneca's 40 million sesterces was a huge sum — enough to bankrupt British tribal elites when he called the loans in.
4In sentence [16], find the TWO words that mean “forced worship of the Roman emperor as a god”.
Answer: imperial cult
Definition: The Roman state religion — worship of the emperor as divine. Britons hated it, and the Temple of Claudius (the centre of this cult in Britain) was one of the first targets in the revolt.
5In sentence [23], find the LATIN word Tacitus used to describe what happened to Boudica's daughters.
Hint: It's in italics (slanted text) — that shows it's a foreign word.
Answer: stuprum
Definition: A Latin legal term for the sexual violation of a person owed citizen-level rights. By using this word, Tacitus (a Roman) signals that Rome was legally and morally in the wrong.
F

Topic Sentences

The opening sentence of every body paragraph

What is a topic sentence?

A topic sentence is the FIRST sentence of a body paragraph. It tells the reader what that paragraph will be about.

A good topic sentence has 2 jobs:
  • Names the cause / idea this paragraph will discuss
  • Connects to the thesis (your main argument)
Why it matters: Topic sentences are signposts. They help the reader follow your argument without getting lost. If a marker can read just your topic sentences and follow your essay, you've nailed structure.
1What is the topic sentence of Body 1 (Political)? Which sentence number is it?
Answer: Sentence [4]
“The first and most structural cause of the revolt was Rome's annexation of the Iceni kingdom and its disregard for the will of King Prasutagus.”
Why this works: It NAMES the cause (annexation), RANKS it (‘first and most structural’ — evaluative language), and KICKS OFF the political argument. Perfect topic sentence.
2What is the topic sentence of Body 2 (Economic)? Which sentence number is it?
Answer: Sentence [9]
“Equally rooted in Roman misgovernance were the economic causes of the revolt — particularly the financial exploitation carried out by Procurator Catus Decianus and the philosopher Seneca.”
Why this works: ‘Equally rooted in Roman misgovernance’ ties this paragraph BACK to the thesis. It names the cause (economic), keeps the argument coherent, and introduces the key people (Decianus and Seneca).
3What is the topic sentence of Body 3 (Cultural / Religious)? Which sentence number is it?
Answer: Sentence [15]
“Beyond the Iceni, the revolt drew its broader power from cultural and religious causes — most notably the Trinovantes' grievances over the Temple of Claudius and Roman land seizures at Camulodunum.”
Why this works: ‘Beyond the Iceni’ widens the focus to a new tribe (the Trinovantes). Names the cause (cultural / religious). ‘Most notably’ is evaluative language — it tells the reader what's most important here.
4What is the topic sentence of Body 4 (Personal)? Which sentence number is it?
Answer: Sentence [21]
“What finally converted these long-building grievances into open revolt was a single personal trigger — the brutal humiliation of Boudica and her daughters.”
Why this works: ‘What finally converted’ signals the climax — this is the moment everything boiled over. ‘Brutal humiliation’ uses evaluative language. Names the cause (personal trigger).

You've made it through all six activities!

You can now spot reporting verbs, cohesive devices, nominalisations, evaluative language, subject-specific terms, and topic sentences in an analytical essay.
