Chunk 1 of 4

The Roman Construction: Barbarian and Foil

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Read the first paragraph aloud and model analytical thinking: “Notice that Tacitus didn’t write about Boudicca because he thought she was great — he used her story to criticise how Rome governed its provinces. So even when he’s sympathetic, he’s not on her side. He’s using her.”

Ask: “If the only person writing your story was your enemy, how might that change what gets recorded?”

In Lesson 1, you were introduced to the concept of historiography — the study of how history is written and interpreted. Lesson 14 returns to that theme with a full case study: how has Boudicca’s image changed from the Roman period to the present day, and what do those changes tell us about the societies that constructed them?

Boudicca is one of the most striking examples in British history of a figure whose meaning has been almost entirely constructed by those who came after her. She left no writings, no recorded speeches in her own words, no monuments of her own making. Every image of Boudicca — from Tacitus’s “wronged queen” to Thornycroft’s imperial warrior, from Victorian nationalist hero to feminist icon — is a projection of later values onto a figure who cannot answer back.

The Roman writers who created the historical record of Boudicca were not neutral observers. Tacitus, writing under the emperors Nerva and Trajan in the late first century AD, used Boudicca as a vehicle for criticising Roman provincial misgovernance. His Boudicca is a wronged queen driven to revolt by legitimate grievances — but she is also “barbarian,” and her revolt is ultimately defeated by Roman military discipline and rational governance. For Tacitus, the revolt’s lesson is not that Romans should liberate conquered peoples, but that they should govern them justly — because injustice produces dangerous instability.

Cassius Dio’s Boudicca, written over a century later, is more sensational. Dio describes her physical appearance in extraordinary detail — red hair, fierce gaze, harsh voice, tall imposing frame — in ways that fit the Roman literary genre of “ethnographic” description, which routinely attributed distinctive physical traits to barbarian peoples to mark them as fundamentally different from Romans. His Boudicca is a figure of spectacle as much as history.

Both representations, despite their differences, share a fundamental structure: Boudicca is allowed heroic or sympathetic qualities, but she remains “other” — a barbarian whose defeat confirms the ultimate rightness of Roman civilisation.

The Medieval Silence and Renaissance Rediscovery

Teacher — Guided Reading (‘We Do’)

Have students read this section silently. Then use think-pair-share: “Why did Boudicca disappear for almost a thousand years, and what brought her back?” (60 seconds). Cold call 2–3 pairs.

Emphasise the connection between political context and historical memory: Henry VIII’s England needed a story about defying Rome.

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire in the late fifth century AD, Boudicca virtually disappeared from historical memory. Medieval European culture drew its sense of British identity from different sources — the Arthurian legend, the Anglo-Saxon foundation myths, Christian martyrology — and had little use for a pagan Celtic warrior queen.

The rediscovery of Boudicca in the sixteenth century was directly connected to the recovery of classical texts after the Renaissance and the growth of English national consciousness. When the Italian humanist Polydore Vergil published his Historia Anglica in 1534, drawing on Tacitus and Dio, he reintroduced Boudicca’s story to English readers for the first time in centuries. The timing was not coincidental: Henry VIII’s England, asserting its independence from Roman papal authority, found in the story of an ancient Briton defying Rome a useful historical precedent.

The Victorian Apotheosis: Queen and Empress

Teacher — Modelling: Irony Analysis

Model the irony explicitly: “A woman who fought against an empire was turned into a symbol for an empire. That’s irony — and it’s the most important point of this section.”

Ask: “Can you think of any modern examples where someone’s story has been used to support something they would have opposed?”

Boudicca’s transformation into a nationalist icon reached its peak in Victorian Britain. The parallels between “Boudicca” (meaning “victory”) and “Victoria” (meaning the same thing in Latin) were irresistible to Victorian commentators. A warrior queen who had defied Rome was a perfect symbol for an empire that, in Victorian eyes, had surpassed Rome.

The Thornycroft statue, commissioned in the 1850s with the involvement of Prince Albert and finally erected at Westminster Bridge in 1902, is the definitive Victorian Boudicca. She stands triumphant in a Roman-style chariot (not a Celtic one), her arm extended in an imperial gesture, her daughters crouching beneath her — a vision of maternal empire. The plinth quotation from Cowper’s 1782 poem — “Regions Caesar never knew / Thy posterity shall sway” — explicitly positions Boudicca as the forerunner of British imperial expansion.

The irony is profound: a woman who fought against imperial conquest was appropriated as a symbol of empire. A Celtic tribal queen was turned into a proto-British nationalist. The statue tells us nothing about the historical Boudicca and everything about the anxieties and ambitions of mid-Victorian Britain.

Secondary Source

Provenance: William Cowper, “Boadicea: An Ode” (1782), stanza 10. Published in Cowper’s Poems (London, 1782). Cowper (1731–1800) was an English poet writing during the reign of George III, at a time of expanding British imperial power. The poem imagines a dying Druid prophesying Britain’s future global empire — using Boudicca’s revolt as the origin point of a triumphant national destiny.

“Regions Caesar never knew
  Thy posterity shall sway,
Where his eagles never flew,
  None invincible as they.”

Historian’s note: Cowper’s poem is a striking example of how representations of Boudicca reveal far more about the era producing them than about the historical figure herself. Writing in 1782 — at the height of British imperial expansion and during the reign of George III — Cowper transforms a Celtic rebel who fought against an empire into a prophet who foresees one. The deep irony is unmistakable: Boudicca, whose revolt was driven by resistance to colonial exploitation, is here recruited to celebrate the very project of empire-building. Note that Cowper uses the name “Boadicea” — a Latinised corruption of the original Brittonic name — which itself signals how far this reimagining is from any authentic Celtic voice. When evaluating this source, students should ask: whose purposes does this representation serve, and what does it tell us about British national identity in the late eighteenth century?

Chunk 4 of 4

Twentieth-Century Reclamations: Feminist and Post-Colonial

Teacher — Scaffolding: Comparing Perspectives

Before reading, set up a comparison: “We’re going to see two more versions of Boudicca. As you read, think about: What does each group want Boudicca to represent? And what are the problems with each approach?”

Think-pair-share after reading: “Which critique — feminist or post-colonial — do you find more convincing, and why?”

The 1970s women’s liberation movement brought a new lens to Boudicca’s story. Feminist historians and writers reclaimed her as a symbol of female resistance against patriarchal and colonial power — a woman who refused to accept the limitations imposed on her sex and her people by a conquering masculine empire. From this perspective, the Roman accounts of her defeat were reread not as evidence of the inevitable triumph of Roman civilisation, but as the suppression of female power by an anxious masculine establishment.

This feminist reclamation was politically valuable but historically complex. The ancient sources do not clearly position Boudicca as a feminist in any modern sense — she fought as a tribal queen defending her people’s honour and land, not as an advocate for women’s rights. Projecting modern feminist values onto her is a form of presentism — interpreting the past through contemporary categories that did not exist at the time.

Post-colonial scholarship from the 1990s onward added a further layer of critique. Scholars began to examine how Boudicca’s image had been used by British imperialists — how the anti-imperial rebel had been turned into an imperialist symbol — and to argue that this appropriation itself reflected the dynamics of colonial power. From a post-colonial perspective, the very act of naming streets, pubs, and regiments after Boudicca in Britain was a form of symbolic violence: absorbing the resistant Other into the imperial narrative and defusing her challenge.

Most recently, Celtic and British identity scholars have argued for the importance of acknowledging Boudicca’s defeat as a colonial tragedy — the destruction of a people and their culture — rather than celebrating her revolt as a romantic story of heroic resistance that ultimately failed. This perspective foregrounds the long-term losses of Celtic culture under Roman and later imperial domination.

Glossary

Click each card to reveal its definition.

Historiography
The study of how historical accounts are created, who creates them, why, and how interpretations change over time.
Presentism
The tendency to interpret the past through the values and categories of the present, often leading to anachronistic conclusions.
Post-colonial
A critical perspective that examines the ongoing effects of colonialism and the ways in which colonial power shapes historical memory and representation.
Appropriation
The act of taking something (an image, a symbol, a person) from its original context and using it for a different, often contrary, purpose.
Apotheosis
The elevation of a person (or concept) to the highest possible status; sometimes used to describe the most extreme glorification of a historical figure.
Feminist Historiography
An approach to history that centres the experiences of women and examines how gender has shaped historical events and their recording.
