Suetonius Paulinus: Racing Against Time

Teacher — Modelling (‘I Do’)

Read aloud and model historical thinking: “Suetonius rode 250 miles with only cavalry. That tells me he understood the urgency — but also that he knew he couldn’t bring his full army in time. This is a commander making calculations under extreme pressure.”

Pause at the decision to abandon Londinium and ask: “What would you have done?” This creates engagement before the analysis.

When news of the revolt reached Suetonius Paulinus on the island of Mona, he acted with characteristic speed. Abandoning his campaign against the Druids, he rode hard for Londinium, covering approximately 250 miles with a cavalry escort, leaving his main force — two legions (XIV Gemina and XX Valeria Victrix) — to follow on foot as quickly as possible.

Tacitus paints the scene of Suetonius’s dilemma with controlled drama. Londinium was not yet a colonia and had no special Roman legal status, but it was already the commercial heart of the province — a bustling trading centre full of merchants, traders, and civilians. The governor arrived to find the town in panic, refugees flooding the streets, and no realistic prospect of defending the settlement with the forces available.

After assessing the situation, Suetonius made the coldly rational decision that would define his reputation for both competence and ruthlessness: he would not attempt to hold Londinium. The town was not defensible with his available forces. To stand and fight on Boudicca’s terms, in ground not of his choosing, with numbers heavily against him, would be to risk the destruction of the entire Roman force in Britain. He ordered the withdrawal of all able-bodied men who could travel, telling those who could not — the elderly, infirm, women who refused to leave — that he could not protect them.

Primary Source: Tacitus, Annals 14.33 (c. AD 98). Tacitus was a Roman senator and historian writing approximately fifty-seven years after the revolt. His father-in-law Agricola served as governor of Britain, giving him access to firsthand accounts of British affairs. Translation: A.J. Woodman (Penguin, 2004).

“Suetonius, however, with wonderful resolution, marched amidst a hostile population to Londinium, which, though undistinguished by the name of a colony, was much frequented by a number of merchants and trading vessels. Uncertain whether he should choose it as a seat of war, as he looked round on his small force of soldiers, and remembered with what a serious warning the rashness of Petilius had been punished, he resolved to save the province at the cost of a single town.”
Historian’s note: This passage reveals Suetonius Paulinus making one of the most ruthless strategic decisions in the history of Roman Britain. Tacitus frames it not as cowardice or indifference, but as cold military logic — saving the province by sacrificing Londinium. The phrase “wonderful resolution” signals Tacitus’s admiration for Suetonius’s discipline, even as the passage acknowledges the human cost. Notably, Tacitus records that those unable to evacuate — the elderly, the infirm, women who could not march — were left to be killed by Boudicca’s forces. This moment marks the psychological turning point of the revolt: Rome had lost three cities and a legion’s infantry, and its governor had chosen strategic retreat over the defence of civilians.

The Destruction of Londinium and Verulamium

Teacher — Guided Reading (‘We Do’)

Have students read both subsections silently. Then use think-pair-share: “Why is the destruction of Verulamium described as a ‘tragic irony’? Discuss with your partner.” (60 seconds). Cold call 2–3 pairs to share.

Key point to draw out: Verulamium was a British town that had adopted Roman culture. Its destruction means the revolt killed pro-Roman Britons, not just Romans.

The Destruction of Londinium

Boudicca’s forces swept into Londinium and destroyed it comprehensively. Tacitus writes that the rebels “did not take or sell prisoners; there was none of the other commerce of war. They were in a hurry to cut throats, hang, burn, and crucify.” Cassius Dio adds the claim that victims were subjected to religious ritual killings, though historians regard this detail with considerable scepticism — it may reflect Roman propaganda designed to cast the rebels as savage barbarians.

The archaeological evidence from Londinium is remarkable in its precision. Excavations across the City of London — particularly those conducted during major construction projects at the Walbrook, the Bloomberg site, and under Leadenhall Market — have revealed a distinctive layer of red-brown burnt debris approximately 30 centimetres thick, dating to the mid-first century AD. This “Boudiccan destruction horizon” contains melted pottery, charred timber, burnt grain, fused glass, and the remains of burned-out buildings.

Key Archaeological Find

The Bloomberg excavation (2010–2014) in the City of London uncovered the largest collection of Roman writing tablets ever found in Britain, alongside abundant evidence of the Boudiccan destruction layer. The burnt horizon at roughly 1–2 metres below current street level directly marks the moment the rebel army passed through the settlement.

Ironically, the Boudiccan destruction of Londinium preserved the archaeological record of the early town remarkably well. The burning sealed the ground surface of AD 60/61 beneath metres of later Roman rebuilding, creating a time capsule of the pre-revolt settlement that continues to yield new discoveries.

Primary Source: Cassius Dio, Roman History (Rhōmaïkē Historia) 62.7, c. AD 211–233. Written approximately 150 years after the Boudiccan revolt. Survives only as an 11th-century Byzantine epitome compiled by the monk Xiphilinus. Translated by Earnest Cary, Loeb Classical Library, 1925.

“The Britons took no prisoners; they could not wait to cut throats, hang, burn, and crucify, as though avenging in advance the retribution that was on its way. They also hung up naked the noblest and most distinguished women and then cut off their breasts and sewed them to their mouths, in order to make the victims appear to be eating them; afterwards they impaled the women on sharp skewers run lengthwise through the entire body. All this they did to the accompaniment of sacrifices, banquets, and wanton behaviour, not only in all their other sacred places, but particularly in the grove of Andate. This was their name for Victory, and they regarded her with most exceptional reverence.”
Historian’s note: This is the only ancient account of atrocities committed against prisoners during the revolt; Tacitus does not describe these acts in comparable detail. Scholars debate whether Dio’s description is historically accurate or rhetorical embellishment — a literary convention for depicting enemy “barbarism” for a Roman audience. The ritual violence described in the grove of Andate follows established Greco-Roman conventions for representing Celtic religious practice. Modern historians including Miranda Aldhouse-Green (Boudica Britannia, 2006) and Richard Hingley and Christina Unwin (Boudica: Iron Age Warrior Queen, 2005) urge significant caution in accepting this passage at face value. Nevertheless, Dio’s account of the scale and ferocity of the destruction is broadly corroborated by the archaeological burn layers identified at Camulodunum, Londinium, and Verulamium.

Dio does not provide the Celtic perspective on events. The epitome problem means we cannot know what Xiphilinus may have omitted, condensed, or altered. Dio’s Greek rhetorical conventions shape his depiction of violence in ways that may exaggerate or distort the historical reality. The passage tells us nothing about the experiences of ordinary people who survived the destruction.

Verulamium: A Tragic Irony

After Londinium, Boudicca’s forces moved on Verulamium (modern St Albans in Hertfordshire), the third major target of the revolt. Verulamium is historically significant because it was not a Roman town in the sense of being a settlement of Roman citizens — it was a British town that had adopted Roman culture. It held municipium status, meaning its leading inhabitants had attained Latin rights.

The destruction of Verulamium thus represents a deeply tragic irony: Boudicca’s revolt, ostensibly conducted in the name of British freedom from Roman oppression, resulted in the massacre of a British community that had chosen to engage with Roman culture. The Trinovantes and other allies may have seen Verulamium’s inhabitants as collaborators; alternatively, the rebel army, having tasted fire and victory, may have been beyond the control of any single commander’s restraint.

Like Camulodunum and Londinium, Verulamium’s destruction is archaeologically attested. A similar burnt layer has been identified across the site, and several pre-Boudiccan buildings have been excavated with their destruction firmly dated to the revolt period. Tacitus records all three towns’ destruction but notably does not dwell on the fate of Verulamium’s residents, perhaps because acknowledging the massacre of pro-Roman Britons complicated his moral framework.

The Scale of the Revolt’s Destruction

Teacher — Scaffolding (‘We Do’)

Read this section aloud and pause at the casualty figures. Ask: “Tacitus says seventy thousand, Dio says eighty thousand. Why might ancient historians disagree on numbers?” This is a key source analysis skill.

Retrieval gesture vote: “Tacitus records 70,000 dead and Cassius Dio records 80,000. True or false: both historians agree on the exact casualty figures. Thumbs up for true, down for false.” (Answer: false — they disagree, which suggests rhetorical exaggeration.) If fewer than 80% correct, highlight the discrepancy. Then think-pair-share: “Why might ancient historians exaggerate casualty numbers?” (60 seconds, cold call.)

Tacitus records that seventy thousand Roman citizens and their allies were killed during the revolt. Cassius Dio inflates this figure to eighty thousand. Neither figure can be independently verified, and ancient writers frequently exaggerated casualty numbers for rhetorical effect. However, the broad picture of massive loss of life across three major urban centres is confirmed by the archaeological evidence. These were not small settlements: Camulodunum had a permanent garrison and a developed urban centre; Londinium was already a thriving commercial town; Verulamium had substantial public buildings.

The economic impact of losing three major towns simultaneously would have been severe. The commercial infrastructure of the province — trading networks, storage facilities, workshops, financial records — was largely concentrated in these urban centres. For the province of Britannia, the Boudiccan revolt represented an economic catastrophe from which recovery took decades.

There is also a human dimension that the sources barely acknowledge. Thousands of ordinary people — traders, craftsmen, slaves, freedmen, and women of all origins — died in the destruction of these towns. Their individual stories are lost. We know them only through the reddish ash layer in the soil.

Written Sources: Archaeological Interpretations

Teacher — Independent Analysis (‘You Do’)

Students read these secondary sources independently. After reading, use think-pair-share: “Choose one of the secondary sources. What does it add to our understanding of the revolt that the primary sources alone do not?” (90 seconds). Cold call 3–4 pairs.

This develops the skill of evaluating how secondary sources build on primary and archaeological evidence.

Secondary Source: Joan P. Alcock, Roman Britain, 2011

“Evidence of the unrest can be noted in the number of hoards that have been discovered. These included 217 silver denarii at Sutton (Suffolk) and 872 silver Iceni coins dated to AD 60–70 at Fring (Norfolk). Metalwork hoards include a large number of silver cups… these were buried either to save them from Boudicca’s rebels or from Roman vengeance.”

Secondary Source: Miranda Aldhouse-Green, Boudica Britannia: Rebel, War-leader and Queen, 2006

“Derek Allen has drawn attention to archaeological evidence for the non-reoccupation of old Icenian settlements after AD 60, something that may well indicate that their inhabitants did not survive to return home. The high-status ‘city’ settlement was abandoned within at most a decade of the Boudican Rebellion: the archaeological record here simply stops. Such total population shifts beg questions as to whether the Icenian inhabitants fled, were massacred or were forcibly dispossessed of their ancestral territory.”

Secondary Source: Sam Moorehead and David Stuttard, The Romans Who Shaped Britain, 2012

“Archaeology provides extensive evidence for the destruction of Camulodunum by Boudicca’s rebels: the burnt remains of walls, painted plaster and roof tiles, along with furniture and quantities of pottery and glassware. Because the buildings were torched, remains of carbonised organic material such as wheat grains, dates and flax have been discovered. Whatever the details, the destruction was wholesale. There is also considerable evidence for Boudican destruction in London, mainly consisting of burnt building debris filled with artefacts and coins dating to the period AD 50–60.”

Secondary Source: Jason Burke, Chief Reporter, The Observer (UK), 2000

“A dig in Colchester has revealed that, when Boudicca’s troops seized the city, they went to enormous lengths to destroy anything touched by the Romans. Many of the houses were built of hardened clay and timber. According to archaeologists, they would have been very difficult to burn. ‘These were not flammable buildings’, said Phillip Crummy, who directed the dig. ‘But they were levelled. It was a murderous, determined, intensive and deliberate attack.’ The dig found that every house had been carefully levelled, one by one, by the Iceni tribesmen.”
