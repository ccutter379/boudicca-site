Chapter 1: Context and Background

Lessons 1–4

Agency L7

The capacity of an individual to act independently and make their own decisions; the power to influence events.

Annexation L4

The formal takeover of a territory by a foreign power.

Bias L1

A tendency to favour one perspective, which can distort the presentation of information.

Celtic L2

Relating to a group of peoples sharing common linguistic, artistic, and religious traditions spread across Iron Age Europe.

Client King L4

A local ruler recognised by Rome in exchange for loyalty, tribute, and cooperation.

Client Kingdom L2

A state that retains internal self-rule in exchange for loyalty, tribute, and military support to a more powerful overlord.

Colonia L3

A Roman colony, often settled by retired soldiers, designed to spread Roman culture and maintain control.

Druid L2

A Celtic religious leader, judge, and educator who transmitted knowledge through oral tradition.

Historiography L1

The study of how history is written, interpreted, and revised across time.

Honour Culture L4

A social system in which status and identity depend significantly on public reputation and the ability to defend one's dignity.

Iceni L2

A Celtic tribe of eastern Britain (modern Norfolk, Suffolk, and parts of Cambridgeshire) and the people led by Boudicca.

Iconography L1

The use of images and symbols to represent particular ideas or figures.

Imperial Cult L3

The worship of the Roman emperor as a divine figure, a key mechanism of political loyalty in the provinces.

La Tène L2

The third and most sophisticated phase of Celtic culture, c. 500 BC–AD 100, characterised by distinctive curvilinear art.

Oral Tradition L1, L2

The practice of passing knowledge, laws, and history down through spoken word, story, and song rather than writing.

Primary Source L1

A source created at or close to the time of the events being studied.

Procurator L3, L4

A senior Roman official responsible for financial administration in a province. Catus Decianus was the procurator who triggered the revolt.

Representation L1

A portrayal or depiction of a person, event, or idea, shaped by the creator's perspective and context.

Resistance L3

Active opposition to Roman rule, ranging from maintaining traditional customs to armed revolt.

Romanisation L3

The process by which conquered peoples adopted Roman language, culture, law, and institutions.

Scourging L4

Flogging or whipping as a punishment. Applying it to Boudicca was a public act of humiliation.

Secondary Source L1

A source created after the events, often interpreting primary sources.

Sestertius L4

A Roman coin; the standard unit of accounting for large sums in the early empire.

Torc L2, L6, L13

A twisted metal neck-ring, typically gold or silver, worn by Celtic elites as a symbol of status.

Tribe L2

A social and political group organised around kinship, shared territory, and loyalty to a common leader.

Trinovantes L3

A Celtic tribe of south-eastern Britain who were displaced by the veteran colony at Camulodunum and became key allies of Boudicca.

Veteran Colony L3

A settlement established for retired Roman soldiers (veterans), usually on confiscated provincial land.

Will (Testament) L4

A legal document stating how a person's property should be distributed after death.

Chapter 2: Sources and the Revolt

Lessons 5–8

Andraste L6

A Celtic goddess invoked by Boudicca in Cassius Dio's account, possibly a goddess of victory or invincibility.

Annals L5

Tacitus's major historical work covering AD 14–68. Boudicca's revolt appears in Book XIV.

Augury L6

The practice of interpreting signs (especially animal behaviour) as omens of future events.

Castra L8

A Roman military camp, built to a standard plan each night on campaign.

Centurion L8

A professional officer commanding a century (80 men) within a Roman legion.

Coalition L7

An alliance of different groups united around a common cause or enemy.

Colonial Resistance L7

Active opposition to the political, economic, or cultural control exercised by an occupying power.

Contemporaneous L5

Occurring at approximately the same time as the events described.

Gladius L8, L11

The Roman legionary's short sword, designed for close-quarters stabbing in tight formation.

Immediate Cause L7

A short-term trigger that directly sets off an event.

Invented Speech L6

A speech composed by a historian for a historical figure, using known events and character as a guide.

Legion L8

The basic large unit of the Roman army, approximately 5,000 heavy infantry organised into ten cohorts.

Logistics L8

The organisation of military supply, including food, equipment, communication, and transport.

Noble Barbarian L5

A literary tradition portraying non-Roman peoples as morally superior to Romans, used to criticise Roman decadence.

Pilum L8, L11

The Roman legionary's heavy javelin, designed to penetrate shields and then bend to prevent reuse.

Rhetoric L5, L10

The art of persuasive speaking or writing. Ancient historians used rhetorical techniques including invented speeches.

Roman History L6

Cassius Dio's major work, written in Greek, covering Roman history from its origins to his own time.

Second Sophistic L6

A cultural movement in the Roman Empire (1st–3rd centuries AD) emphasising Greek rhetoric and literary style.

Senatorial Bias L5

The tendency of Roman senators to criticise emperors who reduced senatorial authority.

Seneca L7

Roman Stoic philosopher and tutor to Emperor Nero who reportedly called in large loans to Iceni nobles.

Testudo L8

The "tortoise" formation: interlocked shields providing protection from missile fire.

Underlying Cause L7

A long-term structural condition that creates the environment in which an event can occur.

War Chariot L8

A light, two-wheeled vehicle used by Celtic warriors for rapid movement and psychological impact.

Chapter 3: The Revolt and Aftermath

Lessons 9–12

Agricola L12

Gnaeus Julius Agricola, governor of Britain AD 77–84, known for advancing Roman occupation and encouraging Romanisation; father-in-law of Tacitus.

Camulodunum L9

The Roman name for modern Colchester, Essex — the first Roman colonia in Britain.

Classicianus L12

Gaius Julius Alpinus Classicianus, procurator of Britain after AD 61, who recommended the recall of Suetonius Paulinus; his tombstone survives in the British Museum.

Conciliation L12

A policy of making peace and reducing hostility rather than continuing military repression.

Defile L11

A narrow pass or valley that limits the width of a military advance.

Destruction Horizon L10, L13

A distinct layer of burnt material in the soil marking a violent destruction event at a specific moment in time.

Formation L11

The arrangement of soldiers in battle; Roman legions used tight, disciplined formations.

Legate L9

A senior Roman officer commanding a legion, typically of senatorial rank.

Legio IX Hispana L9

The Ninth Legion, one of four legions stationed in Roman Britain.

Londinium L10

The Roman name for modern London; the commercial centre of Roman Britain.

Mancetter L11

Modern Warwickshire town and the most widely accepted candidate for the site of Boudicca's final battle.

Mona L9

The Roman name for Anglesey, the last major stronghold of the Druids in Britain.

Municipium L9, L10

A Roman town whose inhabitants held Latin rights, with elected local magistrates.

Poenius Postumus L11

Acting commander of Legio II Augusta who refused to march to Suetonius's support and later committed suicide.

Polyclitus L12

An imperial freedman sent by Nero to investigate the situation in Britain after the revolt.

Stratigraphy L10, L13

The study of geological and archaeological layers (strata) to establish chronological sequences.

Turpilianus L12

The governor who replaced Suetonius Paulinus and adopted a conciliatory policy.

Verulamium L10

The Roman name for modern St Albans; a municipium with a substantial Romanised British population.

Chapter 4: Evidence, Legacy and Skills

Lessons 13–16

Analysis L15

Explaining why something happened, what it means, or what it reveals — going beyond description to interpretation.

Apotheosis L14

The elevation of a person or concept to the highest possible status; extreme glorification of a historical figure.

Appropriation L14

Taking something from its original context and using it for a different, often contrary, purpose.

Contestability L16

The idea that historical interpretations can be legitimately debated and different historians may reach different conclusions.

Description L15

Telling what happened without explaining its meaning or significance — the opposite of analysis.

Essay Scaffold L16

A structured plan outlining the thesis, topic sentences, key evidence, and analytical points for each paragraph.

Feminist Historiography L14

An approach to history that centres the experiences of women and examines how gender has shaped events.

Historical Significance L16

The importance of a person, event, or development in terms of its impact on subsequent events and what it reveals about the period.

Historiographical Significance L16

The importance of how a person or event has been interpreted, debated, and represented by historians across time.

Inference L13

The process of drawing conclusions that go beyond directly observable evidence.

PEEL L15

Point, Evidence, Explain, Link — a paragraph structure for building analytical arguments in essay writing.

Peer Evaluation L16

Assessing a classmate's work using specific criteria and providing constructive feedback.

Post-colonial L14

A critical perspective examining the ongoing effects of colonialism and how colonial power shapes historical memory.

Presentism L14

The tendency to interpret the past through the values of the present, often leading to anachronistic conclusions.

Radiocarbon Dating L13

A scientific dating method that measures the decay of carbon-14 in organic material to determine its age.

Snettisham Treasure L13

A hoard of over 170 gold and electrum torcs found in Norfolk, the most spectacular Iron Age gold find in Britain.

Source Integration L15

The skill of weaving source evidence and evaluation into the flow of an analytical paragraph.

Synthesis L16

Combining evidence, arguments, and perspectives from multiple sources to create a coherent overall interpretation.

Thesis Statement L15, L16

The central argument of an essay — arguable, specific, and directly answering the question.

Topic Sentence L15

The opening sentence of a paragraph that states its main argument or claim (the 'Point' in PEEL).
